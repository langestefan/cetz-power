`diagram(body)` is a thin wrapper around `cetz.canvas` that installs
the `cetz-power` style dictionary into `ctx.style` before invoking
your body. So everything you can do inside a regular CeTZ canvas —
arrows, rectangles, custom paths, math content — works inside a
cetz-power diagram too.

That makes the package easy to extend. When the standard symbols
get you most of the way to the diagram you want but you need an
annotation, a region highlight, or a custom shape, you don't have
to rewrite anything: just drop CeTZ primitives into the same
`diagram` block.

## The CeTZ module is already in scope

The wildcard import re-exports CeTZ alongside the symbols:

```typst
#import "@preview/cetz-power:0.1.0": *
```

That puts `cetz` (the whole module) in your namespace, so you can
reach for `cetz.draw.line`, `cetz.draw.rect`, `cetz.draw.content`,
`cetz.vector.dist`, and anything else from CeTZ without a separate
import. (You can verify this by reading `src/lib.typ` — `cetz` is
re-exported from the deps module.)

The pattern below in each example is the same: take an existing
recipe (the [radial feeder](/cetz-power/recipes/radial-feeder/) is
small enough to keep in your head), copy it, and add CeTZ
primitives to it. Nothing about the cetz-power calls changes.

## Power-flow arrows

A common annotation: show the magnitude and direction of real or
reactive power on a feeder line. `cetz.draw.line` takes a `mark:`
argument for arrowheads at either end:

<Snippet name="extending-cetz-arrow" />

The arrow lives entirely in CeTZ: a line from one absolute coord
to another, with an arrowhead `mark` and a `cetz.draw.content` for
the caption. The cetz-power buses, transformer, and load on the
same canvas are unchanged.

## Region highlights

Mark a study area, a control zone, or a substation boundary with a
dashed rectangle. Same pattern — drop a `cetz.draw.rect` into the
diagram body:

<Snippet name="extending-cetz-region" />

`cetz.draw.rect` takes two opposite corners and accepts the full
CeTZ stroke dict — `(paint:, thickness:, dash:)` — so dashed,
dotted, or coloured boundaries are all one line. Leave `fill:
none` so the symbols underneath stay visible.

## Dimension lines

For as-built drawings, distances and clearances often need to be
called out. A dimension line is two short tick marks at the
endpoints, an arrowed segment between them, and a caption — every
piece is a CeTZ primitive:

<Snippet name="extending-cetz-dimension" />

`mark: (start: "<", end: ">")` puts arrowheads on both ends of the
segment. The tick marks are just two extra short lines. If you
find yourself drawing the same dimension repeatedly, wrap it in
your own `#let` — same pattern as a [custom
symbol](/cetz-power/extending/custom-symbol/), but you don't even
need `symbol()` for non-anchored geometry.

## Other useful CeTZ primitives

A short tour of CeTZ functions that come up most often when
extending a power diagram:

| Function | What it draws |
| --- | --- |
| `cetz.draw.line(a, b, mark: ...)` | Straight segment, optionally with arrowheads. |
| `cetz.draw.rect(corner-a, corner-b)` | Rectangle from two opposite corners. |
| `cetz.draw.circle(center, radius: r)` | Circle. Useful for highlighting a node. |
| `cetz.draw.arc(center, start: a, stop: b, radius: r)` | Arc — handy for indicating angle ranges or rotations. |
| `cetz.draw.bezier(a, b, ctrl1, ctrl2)` | Cubic Bezier. The right call for a curved annotation that needs to wrap around something. |
| `cetz.draw.content(pos, anchor: ..., padding: ..., body)` | Place arbitrary Typst content (text, math, an image) at a coordinate. |
| `cetz.draw.line(..., stroke: (paint:, thickness:, dash:))` | Full stroke dict for colour, thickness, and dash pattern. |

For the full list, see the [CeTZ
documentation](https://github.com/cetz-package/cetz/blob/main/manual.pdf)
— everything in `cetz.draw.*` is fair game inside a `diagram`
block.

## Coordinate compatibility

Every CeTZ coordinate form works inside a cetz-power diagram —
including the cetz-power anchors. `cetz.draw.line` will happily
take `"b1.tap2"` or `bus-frac("b3", 0.25)` as one of its
endpoints, so you can pin a custom annotation directly to a
cetz-power symbol and let it follow when the symbol moves:

<Snippet name="extending-cetz-coords" />

That's the most useful property to remember: there's no boundary
between "cetz-power coordinates" and "CeTZ coordinates" — every
anchor in your diagram is addressable from both sides.
